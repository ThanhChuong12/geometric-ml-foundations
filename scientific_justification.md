# Scientific Justification for Energy-Only Prediction on QM9

This document outlines the scientific rationale for refactoring the NequIP ablation study on the QM9 dataset to an energy-only target ($U_0$) and removing all synthetic forces.

## 1. Physical Nature of the QM9 Dataset

The QM9 dataset is a benchmark database containing geometric, energetic, electronic, and thermodynamic properties of $\approx 134,000$ stable organic molecules. These molecular structures were optimized at the **DFT B3LYP/6-31G(d)** level of theory.

By definition, optimized molecular geometries represent **local minima** on the potential energy surface (PES). At any local minimum (stationary point):
$$\mathbf{F}_i = -\nabla_i E(\mathbf{R}) = \mathbf{0}$$
where $\mathbf{F}_i$ is the force vector acting on atom $i$, $E(\mathbf{R})$ is the total potential energy, and $\mathbf{R}$ represents the atomic coordinates. 

Therefore, the actual physical forces for all molecules in the QM9 dataset are **zero**. 

## 2. Scientific Invalidity of Synthetic Forces

In some machine learning workflows, non-zero "synthetic forces" are generated by adding random Gaussian noise to the coordinates and labeling them with random force vectors, or by using artificial force fields. For a rigorous physical ML model, this approach is invalid for the following reasons:

- **Lack of Physical Grounding**: There is no physical mapping between a molecular configuration and a randomized force vector. Training a neural network to predict random noise forces forces the model to learn a chaotic, non-conservative vector field that contradicts the underlying quantum chemical energy surface.
- **Gradient Consistency Violation**: In physical models like NequIP, forces are defined as the negative gradient of the predicted energy with respect to atomic coordinates:
  $$\mathbf{F}_i = -\frac{\partial E_{\text{pred}}}{\partial \mathbf{r}_i}$$
  If the target energy labels are physical DFT energies (which are smooth and well-behaved) but the target force labels are randomized vectors, the model cannot simultaneously satisfy both targets. The loss function becomes highly conflicted, leading to poor convergence and unphysical energy-gradient relationships.
- **Overfitting to Noise**: Using synthetic forces acts as a source of adversarial noise that degrades the generalization ability of the model on the primary scalar property prediction task.

## 3. Evaluation of E(3)-Equivariance via Scalar Properties

One might ask: *If we only predict a scalar property like $U_0$, does the model still benefit from E(3)-equivariance?*

The answer is **yes**. 

- **Symmetric and Angular Representations**: Even when predicting a scalar invariant under the 3D rotation group $SO(3)$ and translation group $T(3)$ (together forming the Euclidean group $E(3)$), the model must capture the spatial relationships between atoms.
- **Higher-Order Features**: An invariant message-passing GNN ($\ell_{\max}=0$) only computes distances ($r_{ij}$) and cannot capture directional angular relationships (three-body, four-body terms) without expensive explicit triplets. In contrast, an equivariant GNN ($\ell_{\max}=1$) utilizes spherical harmonics to project relative coordinates into vector spaces. These directional features are updated equivariantly throughout the message-passing layers and finally mapped back to scalar invariants at the output.
- **Data Efficiency**: Because equivariant networks naturally incorporate the physics of 3D space, they show much higher sample efficiency. This is reflected in a steeper log-log learning curve (more negative slope) when comparing the equivariant model ($\ell_{\max}=1$) against the invariant baseline ($\ell_{\max}=0$) across different training subset sizes ($N=100$ vs. $N=1000$).

## Conclusion

Refactoring the dataset to an energy-only target aligns the machine learning problem with the actual physics of the QM9 dataset. It provides a clean, rigorous, and scientifically valid benchmark to compare the representation capabilities of invariant and equivariant GNNs.
